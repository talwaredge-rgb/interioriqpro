01

A BoQ has 200+ line items. Most homeowners review it once.

Quantities, unit rates, material specifications, exclusion clauses. Each one matters. Each one is an area where a homeowner without category knowledge is at a disadvantage. Not because the process is designed to deceive, but because this is a specialist document that rewards specialist reading.

Payment milestones should reflect work done, not work promised

Rate Padding

False ceiling: rate 61% above market

₹81,600
What the BoQ says

False ceiling (gypsum board, all rooms): 680 sq ft at ₹195/sq ft = ₹1,32,600

What the market says

Current contractor rate for gypsum board false ceiling in this city range: ₹105–120/sq ft all inclusive (frame, board, finishing, paint-ready). Mid-point: ₹113/sq ft.

Our assessment

The BoQ does not specify whether ₹195 includes the perimeter cove or painting. We asked for clarification; the contractor confirmed it does. At that scope, ₹195/sq ft is 61% above the verified market rate for the same deliverable. Overcharge: 680 sq ft × ₹82 = ₹55,760. Additionally, the BoQ lists "electrical channels in false ceiling" as a separate line item at ₹38/sq ft. This is standard included work at any rate above ₹140. Double-counted value: 680 × ₹38 = ₹25,840. Combined: ₹81,600.

Quantity Inflation

Living room + master bedroom flooring: 94 sq ft overbilled

₹42,300
What the BoQ says

Vitrified tile flooring (600×600mm, living room + master bedroom): 580 sq ft at ₹450/sq ft including tile, adhesive, and laying = ₹2,61,000

What the measurements say

Floor plan measurements (shared by client): Living room 18×14 ft = 252 sq ft. Master bedroom 14×12 ft = 168 sq ft. Wardrobe footprint deducted: 28 sq ft. Net tiling area: 392 sq ft. Standard wastage allowance (8%): 31 sq ft. Adjusted quantity: 423 sq ft.

Our assessment

The BoQ quantity of 580 sq ft exceeds verified area by 157 sq ft. After accounting for the 8% wastage allowance we consider fair, the net overbill is 94 sq ft × ₹450 = ₹42,300. This is a straightforward quantity inflation, not an estimation error. The contractor has the same floor plan the client shared with us.

Double Billing

Waterproofing billed under two separate heads

₹36,000
Line item A (Civil Works section)

"Waterproofing treatment: bathrooms and utility": ₹28,000 lump sum

Line item B (Bathroom section)

"Bathroom waterproofing: 3 coats Dr. Fixit": ₹36,000 lump sum (3 bathrooms)

Our assessment

These are the same scope of work described under different section headings. We asked the contractor to confirm which line covers the Dr. Fixit application. He confirmed both do. One must be removed entirely. At the lower rate, ₹36,000 is the duplicate. The ₹28,000 line at lump sum also warrants a sq ft breakdown before approval. That is a separate negotiation point, not counted in this finding's total.

Spec Ambiguity

Kitchen plywood specified generically: grade unverifiable

₹31,200
What the BoQ says

Kitchen cabinets: "18mm plywood carcass." No brand, no grade (BWP/MR/commercial), no IS standard cited. Quoted at ₹1,100/sq ft for carcass.

Why it matters

BWP (Boiling Water Proof) plywood costs ₹85–95/sq ft. MR (Moisture Resistant) costs ₹52–60/sq ft. Commercial ply costs ₹32–40/sq ft. A kitchen cabinet built with commercial ply instead of BWP will begin delaminating within 3–5 years. The spec as written allows the carpenter to use any grade without breaching the contract.

Our assessment

The kitchen carcass area totals approximately 480 sq ft (verified from layout drawing). The rate difference between BWP (what the customer expects) and commercial ply (what the spec permits) is ₹55–65/sq ft material cost. At ₹65/sq ft: 480 × ₹65 = ₹31,200 is at risk if the carpenter substitutes downward. This is not a confirmed overcharge. It is an unprotected specification that must be corrected in writing before work starts. We have drafted the replacement specification clause.

Rate Padding

Painting: day labour rate 58% above prevailing market

₹27,000
What the BoQ says

Interior painting labour: 18 painter-days at ₹1,900/day = ₹34,200. (Material billed separately.)

What the market says

Prevailing painter day rate in this city: ₹1,100–1,200/day for skilled painter. At ₹1,200: 18 days = ₹21,600.

Our assessment

The contractor is using a day rate labour model but billing at a rate we cannot substantiate for this city and scope. The 18-day estimate for a 1,240 sq ft home is reasonable. The rate is not. Overcharge on labour alone: 18 × ₹700 = ₹12,600. We also note the BoQ does not specify number of coats, primer brand, or sanding requirement. These omissions routinely lead to arguments at execution. We have flagged the three specific clauses that need to be added.

Spec Ambiguity

Main door and bedroom door frames: material unspecified

₹14,400 at risk
Our assessment

The BoQ lists "door frames: 5 nos." at ₹4,800 per frame with no material specification. Teak frames cost ₹6,500–8,000. Sal wood frames cost ₹4,500–5,500. Engineered wood (HDF) frames cost ₹1,800–2,500. The rate quoted is consistent with sal wood. If the client expects teak (as is standard at this project value), the underspecification creates a ₹14,400 gap between quoted and expected. We recommend specifying "Sal wood, Grade A, minimum 3×2 inch section" if the current budget is acceptable, or revising the rate if teak is required.

Expert commentary

This BoQ would pass a casual read. The individual line items are not outrageous, the total is within a plausible range for the scope, and the contractor's reputation is reasonable. The problems only surface when you cross reference quantities against the floor plan, compare rates against current city benchmarks, and read the specification language carefully.

The false ceiling finding is the most important: it combines an inflated rate with a double counted line item in a way that is difficult to spot without knowing what "all inclusive" means for this trade. The waterproofing double billing is the most common pattern we see: same work described under two section headings. The quantity inflation on flooring is a straightforward measurement check that took twelve minutes with a floor plan.

The specification gaps on plywood and door frames are not overcharges yet. They are contract vulnerabilities. Left uncorrected, they give the contractor the legal right to deliver something the client would consider substandard, and to charge for upgrades if the client objects at execution. Fixing these costs nothing at the BoQ stage. It costs significantly more after the carpenter has started work.
